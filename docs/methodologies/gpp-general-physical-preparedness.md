# GPP (General Physical Preparedness)

## Overview

General Physical Preparedness (GPP) is a foundational training methodology focused on building broad, non-specific physical qualities — work capacity, general strength, aerobic fitness, mobility, coordination, and muscular endurance — that serve as the base upon which all specialized training is built. GPP occupies a specific role in the periodization hierarchy: it is the training you do to prepare the body to train effectively, not the training that directly develops sport-specific performance. Every serious periodization model, from linear to block to conjugate, incorporates a GPP phase or component, making it one of the most universal concepts in strength and conditioning.

The methodology contrasts with SPP (Specific Physical Preparedness), which develops the precise physical qualities demanded by a sport or competition. GPP builds the general platform — work capacity, balanced muscular development, aerobic base, joint health, and movement diversity — so that subsequent SPP training can be performed at higher volumes and intensities with lower injury risk. A powerlifter performing sled drags, carries, and upper back hypertrophy work is doing GPP. A marathon runner performing bodyweight strength circuits and mobility work is doing GPP. The common thread is training that improves general physical capacity without directly mimicking competition demands. GPP is not a specific program but rather a training philosophy and programming component that appears within virtually every well-designed training plan.

## Creator/Origin

GPP as a concept emerged from the Soviet sports science tradition of the mid-20th century, where it was formalized as part of the periodization framework developed by Leonid Matveyev and further refined by Yuri Verkhoshansky, Vladimir Zatsiorsky, and others. In the Soviet model, the annual training plan was divided into General Preparation and Specific Preparation phases, with the General Preparation phase explicitly dedicated to building broad physical qualities before transitioning to sport-specific work.

The concept was brought into Western strength training primarily through Louie Simmons at Westside Barbell, who emphasized GPP as a critical component of the conjugate system, famously prescribing sled drags, reverse hypers, and extra workouts to build work capacity alongside maximal strength development. The term gained broader popularity in the CrossFit and functional fitness community, where Greg Glassman's original definition of fitness as "increased work capacity across broad time and modal domains" is essentially a description of GPP as a training goal rather than a preparatory phase. More recently, coaches at EliteFTS, Barbell Medicine, and Mountain Tactical Institute have published practical GPP programming frameworks that bridge the academic concept and real-world application. The methodology continues to be refined as the strength and conditioning field develops a more nuanced understanding of how general fitness qualities transfer to specialized performance.

## Key Principles

**General Before Specific**: Broad physical development must precede specialized training. Attempting to build sport-specific capacity on a weak general foundation limits performance ceiling and increases injury risk.

**Work Capacity Development**: GPP's primary function is expanding the athlete's capacity to tolerate and recover from training volume, enabling more productive SPP phases.

**Movement Diversity**: Exposing the body to a wide variety of movement patterns, loads, tempos, and energy system demands builds robustness and reduces vulnerability to repetitive stress.

**Motor Pattern Foundation**: GPP develops the four fundamental human movement patterns — push, pull, hinge, and squat — along with carries, locomotion, and rotational movements that underpin all athletic activity.

**Muscular Balance**: Addressing weaknesses, asymmetries, and underdeveloped muscle groups that would limit performance or increase injury risk during specialized training.

**Aerobic Base Building**: Developing the cardiovascular and metabolic foundation that supports recovery between sets, between sessions, and across training blocks.

**Joint Health and Tissue Resilience**: Progressive loading of tendons, ligaments, and connective tissue through varied movements and moderate intensities to prepare for the higher demands of specialized training.

**Year-Round Component**: While GPP phases are most prominent during the off-season or general preparation periods, some GPP work should be maintained year-round to prevent detraining of general qualities.

## Programming Structure

**GPP Phase Placement in Annual Plan**:

- **Off-Season (Highest GPP Volume)**: 4-8 weeks of dedicated GPP before transitioning to sport-specific work
- **Pre-Season (Moderate GPP)**: GPP maintained as warm-ups, finishers, and recovery sessions alongside increasing SPP
- **In-Season (Minimal GPP Maintenance)**: Brief GPP elements (10-15 minutes per session) to maintain work capacity and address emerging weaknesses
- **Transition/Recovery (GPP as Primary Training)**: Post-competition periods where GPP work supports active recovery and base rebuilding

**Session Structure Options**:

- **Dedicated GPP Sessions**: 30-45 minute standalone sessions focused entirely on general fitness work
- **GPP as Warm-Up**: 10-15 minutes of movement preparation, mobility, and activation before main training
- **GPP as Finisher**: 10-20 minutes of conditioning, carries, or sled work after main training
- **Extra Workouts**: Low-intensity, short-duration GPP sessions (15-20 minutes) added on recovery days

**GPP Categories and Exercise Selection**:

- **Loaded Carries**: Farmer's walks, sandbag carries, sled drags/pushes, yoke walks
- **Bodyweight Conditioning**: Push-ups, pull-ups, dips, air squats, lunges, burpees
- **Sled and Prowler Work**: Forward drags, backward drags, chest press drags, lateral drags
- **Aerobic Development**: Easy jogging, cycling, rowing, jump rope, swimming
- **Mobility and Movement Quality**: Dynamic stretching, foam rolling, animal flows, yoga-inspired movement
- **Unilateral Work**: Single-leg exercises, single-arm presses and rows, split stance movements
- **Trunk and Core**: Planks, carries, rotational work, anti-extension and anti-rotation exercises

**Volume and Intensity Guidelines**:

- GPP work is performed at low to moderate intensity (RPE 5-7)
- Heart rate should stay in Zone 1-2 for aerobic GPP
- Resistance-based GPP uses moderate loads (40-60% of primary lift maxes) for higher reps
- Emphasis on movement quality and sustained effort over maximal output
- Total GPP volume: 2-4 sessions per week during GPP phases, 15-30 minutes during SPP phases

## Target Audience

**Strength Athletes in Off-Season**: Powerlifters, weightlifters, and strongman competitors between competition cycles who need to rebuild work capacity and address muscular imbalances before the next training block.

**Beginner and Intermediate Athletes**: Developing trainees who need broad physical development before specializing in any specific sport or training modality.

**Team Sport Athletes in Pre-Season**: Football, basketball, soccer, hockey, and other team sport athletes returning from the off-season who need general conditioning before sport-specific training intensifies.

**Military and Tactical Candidates**: Individuals preparing for selection courses, basic training, or tactical fitness assessments that demand broad physical preparedness across multiple domains.

**CrossFit and Functional Fitness Athletes**: Competitors whose sport explicitly demands general physical preparedness — GPP is not just a training phase for CrossFitters but a competition requirement.

**Returning Athletes**: Individuals coming back from injury, illness, or extended time off who need to rebuild general fitness before resuming their normal training programs.

## Advantages and Disadvantages

**Pros**:

- Builds the work capacity foundation that all subsequent training depends on
- Reduces injury risk by addressing muscular imbalances and movement quality deficits
- Improves recovery between training sessions through enhanced aerobic fitness
- Prevents overuse injuries by diversifying movement patterns
- Universally applicable across all sports and training goals
- Low technical demand — most GPP exercises are simple and accessible
- Psychologically refreshing during off-season periods when specificity fatigue is high
- Develops balanced, resilient athletes rather than specialists with fragile support structures

**Cons**:

- Does not directly develop sport-specific performance
- Can feel unproductive to athletes who want to see immediate performance gains in their sport
- Requires discipline to maintain appropriate intensity (athletes often push GPP too hard)
- Difficult to quantify and track compared to sport-specific metrics
- May be perceived as "junk training" by athletes who don't understand its purpose
- Too much GPP during competition phases can interfere with sport-specific peaking
- Requires coaching judgment to determine the right balance of GPP and SPP
- Generic GPP prescriptions may not address an individual's specific weaknesses

## Implementation Guidelines

**Step 1: Assess General Physical Qualities**: Evaluate work capacity, aerobic fitness, mobility, muscular balance, and movement quality to identify deficits.

**Step 2: Identify Specific Weaknesses**: Determine which general qualities are most underdeveloped relative to the athlete's sport demands (e.g., a powerlifter with poor aerobic fitness, a runner with no upper body strength).

**Step 3: Select GPP Modalities**: Choose exercises and activities that address identified weaknesses while maintaining variety — sled work, carries, bodyweight circuits, aerobic conditioning, and mobility work.

**Step 4: Place Within the Training Plan**: Determine whether GPP is a dedicated training phase, an ongoing component of each session, or both, based on the athlete's annual plan and competitive calendar.

**Step 5: Set Appropriate Intensity**: Keep GPP work at RPE 5-7 and Zone 1-2 for conditioning. The purpose is building capacity, not creating additional fatigue that compromises primary training.

**Step 6: Start with Manageable Volume**: Begin with 2-3 GPP sessions per week or 10-15 minute additions to existing sessions and increase gradually.

**Step 7: Track Work Capacity Markers**: Monitor indicators like session completion time, resting heart rate, heart rate recovery, and ability to tolerate training volume increases.

**Step 8: Transition Gradually to SPP**: As the competitive season or specific preparation phase approaches, gradually reduce GPP volume while maintaining a minimal dose for ongoing benefits.

## Variations and Specializations

**Westside GPP**: Sled work, reverse hypers, extra workouts, and high-rep accessory work as prescribed by Louie Simmons within the conjugate system — focused on work capacity for maximal strength development.

**CrossFit-Style GPP**: High-variety functional movements across broad time domains — essentially the original CrossFit methodology applied as general preparation rather than competition.

**Tactical GPP**: Rucking, loaded carries, calisthenics, and sustained aerobic efforts oriented toward military and law enforcement physical demands.

**Endurance Sport GPP**: Strength training, mobility work, cross-training, and core development performed by endurance athletes to build the general physical base that supports running, cycling, or swimming performance.

**Youth Athletic GPP**: Multi-sport exposure, fundamental movement skill development, and general physical literacy for developing athletes before sport specialization.

**Rehabilitation GPP**: Graded general movement exposure during return-to-training periods, progressively rebuilding work capacity, movement quality, and tissue resilience after injury.

**Off-Season Athlete GPP**: Comprehensive general preparation blocks for competitive athletes between seasons, addressing accumulated weaknesses and rebuilding capacity.

## Scientific Basis

**General Adaptation Syndrome (Selye)**: Hans Selye's stress-adaptation model provides the theoretical foundation — GPP applies varied, moderate stressors that develop broad adaptive capacity without exhaustion.

**Transfer of Training**: Research demonstrates that general physical qualities (work capacity, aerobic fitness, muscular balance) positively transfer to specialized performance by improving the body's ability to tolerate and adapt to sport-specific training loads.

**Periodization Theory (Matveyev)**: The original periodization model explicitly includes general preparation phases before specific preparation, based on the principle that broad development precedes specialized development.

**Work Capacity and Training Tolerance**: Studies show that athletes with higher general fitness can sustain greater training volumes during specialized phases, leading to superior long-term performance development.

**Injury Prevention**: Research consistently links muscular imbalances, low work capacity, and poor movement quality to increased injury risk — all targets of GPP training.

**Aerobic Base and Recovery**: The relationship between aerobic fitness and inter-set, inter-session, and inter-block recovery is well-documented, supporting aerobic GPP work even for anaerobic athletes.

**Connective Tissue Adaptation**: Research on tendon, ligament, and cartilage adaptation shows that progressive, varied loading (characteristic of GPP) improves tissue resilience and reduces overuse injury risk.

## Common Mistakes and Solutions

**GPP Too Intense**: Treating GPP sessions like competition training, turning sled drags into maximal sprints and conditioning circuits into all-out efforts. GPP should be moderate intensity (RPE 5-7).

**Insufficient Variety**: Performing the same GPP exercises every session, defeating the purpose of diverse movement exposure and creating new repetitive stress patterns.

**Skipping GPP Entirely**: Jumping directly into heavy, specific training after the off-season without rebuilding work capacity and addressing accumulated weaknesses.

**Too Much GPP During Competition Phase**: Maintaining high GPP volume when the focus should shift to sport-specific preparation, creating unnecessary fatigue.

**Treating GPP as Optional**: Viewing GPP work as "extra credit" rather than a necessary component of long-term athletic development and injury prevention.

**No Progression**: Performing the same GPP work at the same intensity and volume indefinitely without progressive overload, limiting the training effect.

**Wrong Weaknesses Targeted**: Performing generic GPP without assessing individual deficits — a strong aerobic base athlete doesn't need more Zone 2 conditioning as GPP.

**GPP as Punishment**: Using GPP work (burpees, sled pushes) as punishment or hazing rather than purposeful development, creating negative associations and poor execution.

## Integration Applications

**With Linear Periodization**: GPP constitutes the "General Preparation" phase before the "Specific Preparation" and "Competition" phases in the classic Matveyev model.

**With Block Periodization**: GPP work dominates the Accumulation block, building the physical base that the Transmutation block converts into specific performance.

**With Conjugate/Westside**: GPP runs concurrently with max effort and dynamic effort work, performed as extra sessions, sled work, and high-rep accessories to maintain and build work capacity year-round.

**With CrossFit Programming**: CrossFit competition IS GPP testing — the methodology's training approach inherently develops general physical preparedness across multiple domains.

**With Endurance Training**: GPP for endurance athletes includes strength training, mobility, cross-training, and movement diversity that reduces overuse risk and improves performance resilience.

**With Injury Prevention Programs**: GPP overlaps significantly with prehabilitation — the diverse, moderate-intensity movement exposure that reduces injury risk.

**With Youth Development**: The long-term athletic development model (LTAD) recommends GPP-focused training through adolescence before sport specialization, developing well-rounded physical literacy.

**With Return-to-Sport Protocols**: GPP provides the framework for progressively rebuilding general fitness after injury before returning to sport-specific training demands.

## WORKOUT TEMPLATES

**Programming Structure & Ratios:**

- **GPP Phase Duration**: 4-8 weeks during off-season, ongoing 10-20 min per session during other phases
- **Session Distribution**: 2-4 dedicated GPP sessions per week during GPP phase
- **Intensity Range**: RPE 5-7 for resistance-based GPP, Zone 1-2 for aerobic GPP
- **Volume**: Start with 20-30 minutes per session, progress to 30-45 minutes
- **GPP-to-SPP Ratio**: Off-season 70/30, pre-season 40/60, in-season 15/85

**Movement Categories & Exercise Options:**

- **Loaded Carries**: Farmer's walks, sandbag bear hug carry, overhead carry, yoke walk, suitcase carry
- **Sled Work**: Forward drag, backward drag, lateral drag, chest press drag, hand-over-hand rope pull
- **Bodyweight Conditioning**: Push-ups, pull-ups, air squats, lunges, step-ups, dips, burpees, mountain climbers
- **Aerobic Base**: Easy jogging/walking, cycling, rowing, jump rope, swimming
- **Mobility and Movement Quality**: Dynamic warm-up flows, foam rolling, hip circles, thoracic rotation, ankle mobility
- **Unilateral Development**: Single-leg deadlifts, split squats, single-arm rows, single-arm presses
- **Core and Trunk**: Planks, pallof press, suitcase carries, Turkish get-ups, dead bugs, bird dogs

**Time Domains & Workout Formats:**

- **Circuit Format**: 4-6 exercises, 30-45 seconds each, 3-4 rounds, 60 seconds rest between rounds
- **EMOM (Every Minute on the Minute)**: 10-20 minutes, alternating 2-3 exercises
- **Continuous Effort**: 20-30 minutes of sustained moderate-intensity work (sled pushes, rowing, cycling)
- **Tabata/Interval**: 20 sec work / 10 sec rest × 8 rounds per exercise (for bodyweight movements)
- **Carry Complex**: 3-4 carry variations performed back-to-back for distance or time

**Scaling Approaches & Progression Methods:**

- **Volume Progression**: Add 5 minutes per session every 2 weeks during GPP phase
- **Load Progression**: Increase carry and sled weights by 5-10% every 2 weeks
- **Density Progression**: Reduce rest periods between exercises/rounds while maintaining quality
- **Complexity Progression**: Add more challenging movement variations as competence develops
- **Frequency Progression**: Add 1 GPP session per week every 2-3 weeks up to 4 sessions

**Weekly Programming Pattern:**

- **Philosophy**: Build broad physical capacity through diverse, moderate-intensity training that complements sport-specific work
- **Session Placement**: Off-days from primary training, or as warm-ups/finishers within primary sessions
- **Daily Structure**: Brief warm-up → GPP work (circuits, carries, conditioning) → Cool-down/mobility
- **Rest Days**: GPP work is low enough intensity to perform on "rest" days without compromising recovery
- **Monitoring**: Track total session time, loads used, resting heart rate, and subjective recovery

**Example GPP Phase (4 Weeks, Off-Season, 3 Days/Week):**

```
Monday: Carry and Sled Day
Warm-up: 5 min easy row or bike

Circuit (4 rounds, 90 sec rest between rounds):
- Farmer's Walk: 50m @ moderate load
- Sled Push (Prowler): 30m @ moderate load
- Sandbag Bear Hug Carry: 40m
- Backward Sled Drag: 30m

Finish: 10 min easy cycling (Zone 1-2)
Total time: 35-40 minutes

---

Wednesday: Bodyweight and Conditioning Day
Warm-up: 5 min jump rope + dynamic stretching

EMOM 16 minutes:
- Minute 1: 10 Push-ups
- Minute 2: 10 Air Squats
- Minute 3: 8 Pull-ups (or ring rows)
- Minute 4: 12 Lunges (alternating)

Then: 10 min Zone 2 rowing

Finish: 5 min mobility (hip flexors, thoracic spine, ankles)
Total time: 35-40 minutes

---

Friday: Movement Variety Day
Warm-up: 5 min easy jog + animal flow sequence

Circuit (3 rounds, 60 sec rest between rounds):
- Turkish Get-Up: 2 each side @ light-moderate KB
- Single-Leg Romanian Deadlift: 8 each leg @ light DB
- Pallof Press: 10 each side
- Step-Ups: 10 each leg @ bodyweight or light DB
- Band Pull-Aparts: 15 reps

Then: 15 min Zone 2 cycling or incline walk

Finish: 5 min foam rolling
Total time: 40-45 minutes
```

**GPP as Warm-Up (10-15 Minutes Before Main Training):**

```
Option A — Upper Body Day Warm-Up:
- Band Pull-Aparts: 2x15
- Push-ups: 2x10
- Dead Hangs: 2x20 sec
- Arm Circles: 10 each direction
- Light Sled Push: 2x30m
Total: 10 minutes

Option B — Lower Body Day Warm-Up:
- Bodyweight Squats: 2x15
- Hip Circles: 10 each direction each leg
- Walking Lunges: 2x10 each leg
- Glute Bridges: 2x12
- Light Farmer's Walk: 2x40m
Total: 10 minutes
```

**GPP as Finisher (10-20 Minutes After Main Training):**

```
Option A — Sled Finisher:
- Sled Push: 4x40m (moderate load, 60 sec rest)
- Backward Sled Drag: 4x40m (moderate load, 60 sec rest)
Total: 12-15 minutes

Option B — Carry Finisher:
- Farmer's Walk: 3x60m
- Overhead Carry: 3x40m (light KB)
- Sandbag Carry: 2x40m
Total: 10-12 minutes

Option C — Conditioning Finisher:
- Assault Bike or Rower: 10 min Zone 2
  OR
- Jump Rope: 5 rounds of 2 min on / 30 sec off
Total: 10-12 minutes
```

**GPP for Strength Athletes (Year-Round Maintenance):**

```
Off-Season: 3 dedicated GPP sessions/week + warm-ups
Pre-Season: 2 GPP sessions/week (reduced to finishers)
In-Season: GPP warm-ups only (10-15 min before each session)
Post-Competition: Return to off-season GPP protocol for 2-4 weeks

Minimum Effective Dose (In-Season):
- Sled work: 2x/week, 10 min per session
- Carries: 1x/week, 5-10 min
- Mobility: Daily, 5-10 min
```

**GPP Progression Across 8-Week Off-Season Block:**

```
Weeks 1-2: Introduction
- 2 sessions/week, 25-30 min each
- Light loads, learning movement patterns
- RPE 5-6 on all work

Weeks 3-4: Volume Build
- 3 sessions/week, 30-35 min each
- Moderate loads, add exercise variety
- RPE 6-7 on all work

Weeks 5-6: Capacity Push
- 3-4 sessions/week, 35-40 min each
- Increase carry distances and sled loads by 10%
- RPE 7 on most work, brief pushes to RPE 8

Weeks 7-8: Transition to SPP
- Reduce to 2 GPP sessions/week
- Increase sport-specific training volume
- GPP becomes warm-up/finisher format
- RPE 5-6 (maintenance intensity)
```
