# RPE / Autoregulation-Based Programming (Tuchscherer / Barbell Medicine)

## Overview

RPE (Rating of Perceived Exertion) and Autoregulation-Based Programming is a training methodology that replaces fixed percentage-based prescriptions with effort-based intensity targets, allowing athletes to adjust training loads in real time based on daily readiness and performance. The system uses the modified Borg RPE scale (typically 1-10, where 10 represents absolute maximal effort and failure) or its derivative, Reps in Reserve (RIR), to prescribe and regulate training intensity. Rather than dictating that an athlete squat 85% of their 1RM, the program prescribes an RPE 8 (2 reps in reserve), and the athlete selects the load that matches that effort level on that particular day.

This approach solves a fundamental problem with percentage-based programming: a lifter's true daily capacity fluctuates by 5-15% based on sleep, stress, nutrition, accumulated fatigue, and recovery status. Percentage-based programs cannot account for this variability, leading to sessions that are either too easy (when the athlete is having a good day) or too hard (when recovery is compromised). Autoregulation captures this variability automatically, ensuring the training stimulus remains productive across good and bad days alike. The methodology has been adopted across powerlifting, strength sports, and evidence-based fitness programming, and is increasingly integrated into hybrid and periodized approaches as a load-selection mechanism.

## Creator/Origin

The modern application of RPE to resistance training was pioneered by Mike Tuchscherer, a competitive powerlifter and coach who founded Reactive Training Systems (RTS) in the late 2000s. Tuchscherer adapted Gunnar Borg's original 6-20 RPE scale (developed in the 1960s for cardiovascular exercise) into a resistance training-specific 1-10 scale, where each number corresponds to a number of reps the athlete could have performed before failure. His system, which he termed "Reactive Training," introduced concepts like RPE-based load selection, fatigue drops (reducing weight by a fixed percentage after a top set to accumulate volume at a controlled effort), and autoregulated periodization.

The methodology was further developed and popularized by Barbell Medicine, founded by Dr. Jordan Feigenbaum and Dr. Austin Baraki, both physicians and competitive strength athletes. Barbell Medicine synthesized Tuchscherer's RPE system with evidence-based programming principles, stress-recovery-adaptation theory, and practical coaching experience to create accessible templates and educational content that brought autoregulation to a broader audience beyond competitive powerlifters. The RIR (Reps in Reserve) framework, which maps directly to RPE (RPE 8 = 2 RIR, RPE 9 = 1 RIR, etc.), was adopted by researchers like Eric Helms and the evidence-based fitness community as the standard language for prescribing proximity to failure in both strength and hypertrophy contexts.

## Key Principles

**Daily Readiness Accommodation**: Training loads are determined by the athlete's capacity on any given day, not by a fixed percentage of a historical max that may not reflect current performance.

**RPE as Intensity Prescription**: Instead of prescribing a specific weight, the program prescribes an effort level (e.g., RPE 8 for 5 reps), and the athlete works up to the load that matches that effort.

**Reps in Reserve (RIR)**: A direct translation of RPE where the athlete estimates how many more reps they could perform before failure (RPE 8 = 2 RIR, RPE 9 = 1 RIR, RPE 10 = failure).

**Fatigue Management Through Effort Control**: By capping effort at prescribed RPE levels, the system prevents the excessive fatigue accumulation that occurs when percentage-based programs demand loads the athlete cannot productively handle.

**Skill Development**: Accurate RPE assessment is a skill that improves with practice. New users typically underestimate or overestimate effort and require 4-8 weeks of calibration.

**Progressive Overload Through Load PR Tracking**: Rather than adding fixed weight weekly, progress is measured by the load achieved at a given RPE over time (e.g., "my RPE 8 squat for 5 reps went from 315 to 335 over 6 weeks").

**Fatigue Drops and Back-Off Sets**: After a top set at the prescribed RPE, subsequent sets are performed at reduced loads (typically -5 to -10%) to accumulate volume without exceeding the fatigue budget.

**Compatibility with Any Periodization Model**: RPE is a load-selection mechanism, not a periodization model. It can be used within linear, block, DUP, conjugate, or any other periodization framework.

## Programming Structure

**RPE Scale for Resistance Training**:

- RPE 6: Could do 4+ more reps; light, warm-up feel
- RPE 7: Could do 3 more reps; moderate effort, starting to work
- RPE 8: Could do 2 more reps; challenging but controlled
- RPE 9: Could do 1 more rep; very hard, near-maximal effort
- RPE 9.5: Maybe could do 1 more rep; maximal grind
- RPE 10: Absolute failure; no more reps possible

**Basic Session Structure**:

- Warm-up: Progressive singles/doubles working up to working weight
- Top Set(s): Work to prescribed RPE at target rep range
- Back-Off Sets: Reduce load by 5-10%, perform prescribed volume sets
- Accessory Work: RPE 7-8, less precise autoregulation needed

**Fatigue Drop Protocol**:

- Perform top set at prescribed RPE (e.g., 5 reps @ RPE 8 = 335 lbs)
- Drop weight by 5% for back-off sets (335 × 0.95 = ~315 lbs)
- Perform 3-5 back-off sets at the reduced weight
- Stop if RPE exceeds the target on back-off sets (fatigue signal)

**Weekly Progression Model**:

- Week 1: Top sets @ RPE 7, back-off sets @ RPE 6-7
- Week 2: Top sets @ RPE 8, back-off sets @ RPE 7
- Week 3: Top sets @ RPE 8-9, back-off sets @ RPE 7-8
- Week 4: Deload — top sets @ RPE 6, reduced volume

**Rep Range Rotation (DUP-Compatible)**:

- Day 1: Heavy (3-5 reps @ RPE 8-9)
- Day 2: Moderate (6-8 reps @ RPE 7-8)
- Day 3: Light/Volume (8-12 reps @ RPE 7)

## Target Audience

**Intermediate to Advanced Strength Athletes**: Lifters who have developed sufficient self-awareness to assess effort accurately and who have plateaued on beginner linear progression programs.

**Competitive Powerlifters**: The primary population for whom RPE-based programming was developed, allowing precise fatigue management across competition preparation cycles.

**Evidence-Based Fitness Practitioners**: Coaches and athletes who value research-backed approaches and want a principled method for load selection within any periodization framework.

**Busy Professionals**: Trainees whose recovery varies significantly due to work stress, travel, sleep disruption, and other life demands that make fixed-percentage programs unreliable.

**Masters Athletes**: Older lifters whose recovery capacity and daily variability make autoregulated load selection more practical than rigid percentage-based programming.

**Hybrid Athletes**: Athletes training both strength and endurance whose strength performance fluctuates based on endurance training fatigue, making daily autoregulation essential.

## Advantages and Disadvantages

**Pros**:

- Automatically accounts for daily performance variability
- Prevents overreaching on bad days and undertraining on good days
- Compatible with any periodization model or training split
- Reduces injury risk by scaling loads to daily capacity
- Develops athlete self-awareness and training intuition over time
- Enables meaningful progress tracking through load-at-RPE comparisons
- Supported by research showing equivalent or superior outcomes to percentage-based programs
- Practical for athletes with variable schedules, stress, and recovery

**Cons**:

- Requires 4-8 weeks of calibration for accurate self-assessment
- Beginners lack the experience to rate effort accurately
- Can enable sandbagging (consistently underrating effort to avoid hard work)
- Lacks the simplicity and objectivity of percentage-based programs
- Requires honest self-assessment — ego lifting undermines the system
- Some athletes perform better with the external accountability of fixed weights
- Harder to program in group or team settings where individual assessment varies
- RPE for isolation and higher-rep work is less precise than for heavy compounds

## Implementation Guidelines

**Step 1: Learn the RPE Scale**: Study the 1-10 RPE scale and its RIR equivalents. Understand what each number feels like conceptually before attempting to apply it.

**Step 2: Calibrate Over 4-8 Weeks**: During a transition period, program both percentage targets and RPE targets. Compare what the percentage says the load should be with what the RPE says it feels like. This dual-tracking builds calibration.

**Step 3: Start with Compounds Only**: Apply RPE to primary compound lifts (squat, bench, deadlift, overhead press) where effort assessment is most reliable. Use standard rep/set prescriptions for accessories.

**Step 4: Use Conservative RPE Targets Initially**: Start with RPE 7-8 prescriptions rather than RPE 9-10 until calibration is established. Undertrained sets are better than overtrained sets during the learning phase.

**Step 5: Log Extensively**: Record weight, reps, RPE, and any notes on daily readiness for every working set. This data is essential for tracking progress and improving calibration.

**Step 6: Implement Fatigue Drops**: After top sets, use fatigue drops (-5 to -10%) for back-off volume. This is the core volume accumulation strategy in RPE-based programs.

**Step 7: Track Load-at-RPE Over Time**: Progress is measured by the weight achieved at a given RPE and rep count. If RPE 8 × 5 was 315 last month and 325 this month, that is objective progress.

**Step 8: Integrate with Periodization**: Use RPE within your chosen periodization framework (linear, DUP, block) by varying target RPEs across the mesocycle rather than varying percentages.

## Variations and Specializations

**Reactive Training Systems (RTS)**: Tuchscherer's original framework with sophisticated fatigue management, pivot blocks, and competition peaking protocols for competitive powerlifters.

**Barbell Medicine Templates**: Accessible RPE-based programs (The Bridge, Strength I/II/III) that combine autoregulation with evidence-based programming for a broad audience.

**Flexible DUP with RPE**: Daily undulating periodization where rep ranges vary by day and load is selected via RPE, allowing both variation and autoregulation simultaneously.

**RPE with Velocity Backing**: Combining subjective RPE with objective velocity-based training (VBT) data to improve load selection accuracy and provide external validation of effort.

**RPE for Hypertrophy (RIR-Based)**: Using RIR prescriptions (e.g., "3 sets of 10-12 at RIR 2") within hypertrophy-focused mesocycles, as popularized by Renaissance Periodization and evidence-based bodybuilding.

**Session RPE**: Rating overall session difficulty (1-10) to track training load over time and manage weekly/monthly fatigue accumulation.

**Team Sport Autoregulation**: Adapting RPE concepts for team environments where athletes self-select loads within prescribed ranges based on individual readiness.

## Scientific Basis

**Rating of Perceived Exertion (Borg)**: Gunnar Borg's original research establishing that subjective effort ratings reliably correlate with physiological markers of exercise intensity.

**Autoregulation Efficacy**: Studies comparing autoregulated to fixed-load programs show equivalent or superior strength gains, with reduced overreaching and better fatigue management.

**RIR Accuracy**: Research demonstrates that trained lifters can estimate RIR within 1 rep with reasonable accuracy, particularly at higher intensities (RPE 8+) and on compound lifts.

**Daily Readiness Variability**: Studies document 5-15% fluctuations in daily strength capacity based on sleep, stress, nutrition, and accumulated training fatigue, validating the need for autoregulation.

**Proximity to Failure**: Research shows that strength gains are similar across a range of proximities to failure (RPE 7-10), while hypertrophy may benefit from closer proximity (RPE 8-10), supporting RPE-based prescription for both goals.

**Fatigue Monitoring**: Session RPE and RPE-based load tracking correlate with objective fatigue markers (HRV, performance decrements), providing a practical monitoring tool without laboratory equipment.

**Periodization Compatibility**: Research supports that autoregulation can enhance outcomes within any periodization model by ensuring daily loads match daily capacity.

## Common Mistakes and Solutions

**Sandbagging**: Consistently underrating effort to avoid challenging sets. Solution: periodically validate RPE with actual rep-out tests or velocity data.

**Ego Lifting**: Overrating capacity and selecting loads heavier than the prescribed RPE, leading to excessive fatigue. Solution: film sets and review — if bar speed is grinding on an "RPE 8," calibration is off.

**Applying RPE to Everything**: Trying to precisely autoregulate isolation exercises and high-rep sets where effort assessment is inherently imprecise. Solution: reserve strict RPE for primary compounds; use general effort ranges for accessories.

**Skipping the Calibration Period**: Jumping into RPE-based programming without the 4-8 week learning phase. Solution: run a dual-system transition where both percentages and RPE are tracked.

**Ignoring Volume**: Focusing only on top-set RPE while neglecting total volume. Solution: use fatigue drops and prescribe minimum back-off set counts to ensure adequate volume accumulation.

**No Progress Tracking**: Failing to log load-at-RPE data, making it impossible to assess whether strength is improving. Solution: track top-set weight at target RPE for each primary lift across mesocycles.

**Inconsistent Scale Usage**: Using different internal standards for RPE on different days. Solution: anchor RPE to specific, concrete experiences (e.g., "RPE 9 is when the bar slows noticeably on the concentric").

**Avoiding High RPE**: Never training above RPE 8, missing the stimulus from genuinely heavy sets. Solution: periodize RPE targets to include RPE 9-9.5 sets during intensification phases.

## Integration Applications

**With Daily Undulating Periodization**: RPE provides the load-selection mechanism for DUP's daily intensity variation, ensuring each rep range target is hit at the appropriate effort level.

**With Block Periodization**: RPE targets can be modulated across blocks — lower RPE during accumulation, higher RPE during transmutation/realization.

**With Renaissance Periodization**: RIR (the inverse of RPE) is the standard intensity prescription in RP's mesocycle model, progressing from RIR 3-4 to RIR 0-1 across the mesocycle.

**With Hybrid Training**: Autoregulation is essential for hybrid athletes whose strength performance fluctuates based on endurance training volume and fatigue.

**With Velocity-Based Training**: VBT data (bar speed at given loads) provides an objective check on subjective RPE, improving calibration and catching sandbagging or ego lifting.

**With Competition Preparation**: RPE-based peaking allows athletes to hit heavy loads (RPE 9-9.5) during the final weeks without being locked into potentially inappropriate percentages.

**With Injury Return-to-Training**: RPE allows athletes returning from injury to train at appropriate intensities based on actual daily capacity rather than pre-injury percentages that no longer apply.

**With Team Training**: Coaches can prescribe effort targets that allow individuals within a group to train at appropriate loads without requiring individualized percentage calculations for each athlete.

## WORKOUT TEMPLATES

**Programming Structure & Ratios:**

- **Session Structure**: Warm-up → Top Set(s) at prescribed RPE → Back-off sets at fatigue drop → Accessories
- **Primary Lift RPE Range**: RPE 7-9 depending on mesocycle phase
- **Back-Off Set Protocol**: Top set weight minus 5-10%, 3-5 additional sets
- **Accessory RPE**: General RPE 7-8, less precise autoregulation
- **Mesocycle RPE Progression**: Week 1 RPE 7 → Week 2 RPE 7-8 → Week 3 RPE 8-9 → Week 4 Deload RPE 6

**Movement Patterns & Exercise Preferences:**

- **RPE-Regulated Movements**: Squat, bench press, deadlift, overhead press (compounds where RPE is most reliable)
- **Fixed-Prescription Movements**: Isolation and accessory work (standard rep/set ranges)
- **Movement Hierarchy**:
  1. Primary compound lift — autoregulated top set + fatigue drops
  2. Secondary compound variation — RPE-regulated, lower intensity
  3. Accessory movements — standard prescription, RPE 7-8 general effort
- **Exercise Selection**: Choose movements where you can reliably assess effort — avoid novelty exercises for RPE regulation

**Time Domains & Workout Formats:**

- **Session Duration**: 60-90 minutes
- **Warm-up**: 10-15 minutes of progressive loading toward working weight
- **Top Set Phase**: 10-20 minutes (working up + top set)
- **Back-Off Phase**: 15-25 minutes (volume accumulation)
- **Accessory Phase**: 15-25 minutes
- **Rest Periods**: 3-5 minutes for primary compounds, 2-3 minutes for back-offs, 1-2 minutes for accessories

**Scaling Approaches & Progression Methods:**

- **Load-at-RPE Tracking**: The primary progress metric — record the load achieved at target RPE and rep count
- **Fatigue Drop Percentage**: -5% for advanced lifters with good calibration, -10% for intermediate lifters
- **RPE Ceiling Progression**: Start mesocycle at RPE 7, progress to RPE 9 over 3-4 weeks
- **Volume Progression**: Add back-off sets across the mesocycle (3 sets week 1 → 5 sets week 3)
- **Deload Protocol**: Reduce RPE to 6, reduce volume by 40-50%, maintain frequency

**Weekly Programming Pattern:**

- **Philosophy**: Prescribe effort, not weight — let daily capacity determine the load
- **Daily Structure**: 1 primary compound (autoregulated) + 1 secondary compound + 2-3 accessories
- **Training Days**: 3-4 per week for most lifters
- **RPE Calibration**: Film top sets weekly to validate effort assessment
- **Logging**: Weight, reps, RPE, and daily readiness notes for every working set

**Example Program (4-Day Upper/Lower with RPE):**

```
WEEK 1 (Introduction — RPE 7)

Monday: Lower Body A
- Back Squat: Work to top set of 5 @ RPE 7
  Fatigue drop: -5%, 4x5
- Romanian Deadlift: 3x8 @ RPE 7
- Leg Press: 3x10-12
- Leg Curl: 3x10-12
- Ab Wheel: 3x10

Tuesday: Upper Body A
- Bench Press: Work to top set of 5 @ RPE 7
  Fatigue drop: -5%, 4x5
- Barbell Row: 3x8 @ RPE 7
- Overhead Press: 3x8 @ RPE 7
- Face Pulls: 3x15
- Tricep Extension: 3x12

Thursday: Lower Body B
- Deadlift: Work to top set of 5 @ RPE 7
  Fatigue drop: -5%, 3x5
- Front Squat: 3x6 @ RPE 7
- Bulgarian Split Squat: 3x8 each leg
- Leg Curl: 3x10-12
- Pallof Press: 3x10 each side

Friday: Upper Body B
- Overhead Press: Work to top set of 5 @ RPE 7
  Fatigue drop: -5%, 4x5
- Weighted Pull-ups: 3x6 @ RPE 7
- Close-Grip Bench: 3x8 @ RPE 7
- Cable Row: 3x10
- Lateral Raise: 3x12-15

---

WEEK 2 (Building — RPE 7-8)

Same structure, but:
- Top sets @ RPE 8 (expect 5-15 lbs more than Week 1 for same RPE)
- Fatigue drops: -5%, 4x5 (same volume)
- Accessories: slight increase in load or reps

---

WEEK 3 (Peak Training Week — RPE 8-9)

Same structure, but:
- Top sets @ RPE 8-9
- Fatigue drops: -5%, 5x5 (add 1 back-off set)
- Accessories: maintain or slight increase

---

WEEK 4 (Deload — RPE 6)

Same exercises, but:
- Top sets @ RPE 6 (significantly lighter — this should feel easy)
- Back-off sets: 2x5 only (50% reduction in volume)
- Accessories: 2 sets instead of 3, reduced load
```

**RPE-to-Percentage Approximation Table:**

```
RPE  | RIR | ~% of 1RM (for 1 rep) | ~% of 1RM (for 5 reps)
-----|-----|-----------------------|------------------------
10   | 0   | 100%                  | 87%
9.5  | 0-1 | 97-98%                | 85%
9    | 1   | 95-96%                | 83%
8.5  | 1-2 | 93-94%                | 81%
8    | 2   | 90-92%                | 79%
7.5  | 2-3 | 88-89%                | 77%
7    | 3   | 85-87%                | 75%
6    | 4   | 80-83%                | 70%
```

**Fatigue Drop Examples:**

```
Top Set: Squat 365 x 5 @ RPE 8
-5% Drop: 365 x 0.95 = 345 lbs
Back-off sets: 4 x 5 @ 345 lbs

Top Set: Bench 255 x 5 @ RPE 8
-5% Drop: 255 x 0.95 = 240 lbs
Back-off sets: 4 x 5 @ 240 lbs

Top Set: Deadlift 425 x 3 @ RPE 9
-7% Drop: 425 x 0.93 = 395 lbs
Back-off sets: 3 x 3 @ 395 lbs
```

**Competition Peaking with RPE (8-Week Example):**

```
Week 1-2: Volume Phase
- Primary lifts: Top set of 5 @ RPE 7-8, 4-5 back-off sets
- Focus: volume accumulation, technique refinement

Week 3-4: Transition Phase
- Primary lifts: Top set of 3 @ RPE 8, 3-4 back-off sets
- Reduce accessory volume, maintain compound quality

Week 5-6: Intensity Phase
- Primary lifts: Top set of 2 @ RPE 8-9, 2-3 back-off sets
- Competition movements only, minimal accessories

Week 7: Overreach
- Primary lifts: Top singles @ RPE 9-9.5
- Minimal volume — 1-2 back-off sets only
- This week should feel hard; fatigue is intentional

Week 8: Taper
- Monday: Openers (RPE 8 singles) — final heavy session
- Wednesday: Bar work and light movement only
- Saturday/Sunday: Competition
```

**Calibration Drill (Monthly):**

```
Select a primary compound lift (e.g., squat)
1. Work up to a set of 5 at what you believe is RPE 8
2. Record the weight
3. Rest 5 minutes
4. Attempt a max set at the same weight (go to actual failure)
5. Count total reps achieved

If you got 7 reps (5 reps + 2 more): RPE 8 was accurate
If you got 9 reps (5 reps + 4 more): You underestimated — that was RPE 6
If you got 6 reps (5 reps + 1 more): You overestimated — that was RPE 9

Use this data to recalibrate your internal RPE scale
```

**Readiness-Based Session Adjustment:**

```
Daily Readiness Score: Rate 1-10 based on sleep, stress, energy, soreness

Score 8-10 (Great day):
- Follow programmed RPE targets
- May exceed expected loads — let it happen naturally
- Good day for top-end intensity work

Score 5-7 (Average day):
- Follow programmed RPE targets
- Loads may be 5-10% lower than best days — this is normal
- Complete all prescribed volume

Score 3-4 (Below average):
- Reduce RPE targets by 1 point (e.g., RPE 8 → RPE 7)
- Complete main work, reduce or skip accessories
- Prioritize movement quality over load

Score 1-2 (Bad day):
- Light movement only or rest
- If training: RPE 6 cap, primary lifts only, no back-off sets
- Focus on recovery for next session
```
